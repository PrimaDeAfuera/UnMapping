Why UnMapping?

Unmapping is a practice of illuminating landscapes that are erased, un-chartable, and/or otherwise unmapped by contemporary and mainstream maps and mapping practices. This includes maps and methods used across various scholarly and political sectors like environmental planning, spatial analyses of energy equity, and cultural narratives of history and place. The map below, centered around the archipelago of Borinkén, lives alongside existing unmapping projects that highlight the rich legacies of survivance of Afro and Indigenous peoples in the wake of the interconnected forces of climate change (ecocide) and racist settler-colonialism (genocide) within the so-called US territories, in the Caribbean, and in imaginaries* beyond.

The map below is centered on the Maricao State Forest. This project is partially inspired by an archival document submitted to the US Department of the Interior's National Register of Historic places. This submission was filed by a group of Puerto Ricans working in anthropology, journalism, history, and conservation architecture and describes the relationship between the state forests and historic Spanish settler-colonial conservation practice. A question that came up for me while reading these document: what are the benefits of "state" level conservation for Afro and Indigenous residents within a US-territory (colony?). Do these efforts improve local health outcomes and/or; deepen traditional kinships with land?; support Afro and Indigenous ecological practices? As I sit with these questions and continue resarchign aroudn them, so too will this map change in response to what I know. Stay tuned for the changes primxs (wherever you may be).
